Making data consolidation feel safe.

Designing the Supplier Details, Merge & Split flows for an AI-powered supplier master data platform.

Role: Collaborating Designer Tool: Figma Timeline: ~3 weeks Status: Phase 1 Complete

> 01 · THE_PROBLEM

The gap wasn't technical.
It was human.

Enterprise procurement teams deal with supplier data coming in from multiple systems — ERPs, CRMs, spreadsheets. The result is a bloated supplier database full of duplicates, incorrectly merged records, and tangled relationships. The existing approach was largely manual and rule-based.

No governed merge flow.

Merges happened without structure or traceability. Data stewards had no guided process.

No explicit relationship assignment.

Subsidiaries and affiliates moved silently — or not at all — when suppliers were consolidated.

No way to undo a wrong consolidation.

Once merged, there was no safe path back. Mistakes required engineering intervention.

Data stewards were making irreversible decisions with no structure and no safety net.

Platforms existed.
None solved this for humans.

Platforms like Informatica MDM, SAP Master Data Governance, and Reltio exist — but they're built for IT administrators, not business users. Their merge workflows are buried in configuration panels with no visual feedback and no explicit relationship handling.

> 03 · USERS

One shared need
across all roles.

The platform serves data stewards, procurement managers, and operations admins. What they share isn't a job title — it's a need for confidence. Every action they take has downstream consequences they may not fully see.

Data Stewards

Manages record quality daily. Makes the most consequential decisions on the platform.

Procurement Managers

Ops Admins

Configures and maintains the platform. Needs visibility across all supplier hierarchies.

They didn't need faster. They needed trustworthy.

> 04 · PROCESS

The scope wasn't
in the PRD.

Merge and split weren't scoped initially. The first stakeholder call revealed the real need — there was no governed way to handle supplier consolidation. No competitor had solved this for business users. I had to design from scratch with no prior reference.

Front-loaded the thinking.

Read the PRD thoroughly, understood the why before touching Figma. No wireframes before the problem was sharp.

Discovered in the field.

Merge and split were uncovered in the first stakeholder call — not in the brief. The best problems aren't always in the PRD.

One real iteration.

Everything else was right the first time because the thinking happened upfront. The only change was relationship assignment logic.

> 05 · SUPPLIER_DETAILS_PAGE

The launchpad
for everything.

Before a user can merge or split a supplier, they need to understand it fully. The details page is where that understanding happens — and the first design problem was information architecture.

A supplier record holds contacts, locations, documents, relationships, and a full audit log. The challenge was surfacing the right information without overwhelming.

Tab architecture separates concerns — each tab has one job. Users get what they need without scrolling through everything at once.

Header strip surfaces status, classification, DUNS ID, and Auto Sync state at a glance — before any scrolling.

Auto Sync badge signals the AI is maintaining this record — reducing manual intervention anxiety.

The edit state keeps users in context. No modal takeover, no page redirect — editing happens inline. Users can update contacts, add locations, upload documents, and confirm without losing their place in the record.

Inline editing preserves spatial memory. Cancel/Confirm always visible — no scroll to save.

> CHAPTER · MERGE_FLOW

Merge Flow: What the design had to guarantee.

> 06 · MERGE_FLOW

Four steps.
No surprises. Nothing moves silently.

Merging two supplier records is one of the most consequential actions in the platform. I designed a four-step guided flow that makes every decision visible and every action deliberate.

No separate flow. No context switch. The action lives where the data lives.

Step 1 — Add Target Supplier

The user identifies the supplier to merge into. Source and target are clearly labelled with key identifiers — DUNS ID, classification, industry, location — visible on both cards. No blind merges.

Step 2 — Select Merge Strategy

Early designs used a dropdown for strategy selection. Users didn't understand what they were choosing until after they'd chosen it. The fix was a card-based layout with plain-language descriptions of exactly what happens to both suppliers.

Merge A into B

Target supplier remains the Golden Supplier Record. Source is absorbed and becomes historical.

Create a new Golden Record

Both suppliers become historical. A new GSR is created from the combined data — user names it.

Consequences before commitment — not after.

Step 3 — Compare and Edit

Source and target attributes are shown in two columns. Users select which value carries forward — field by field, for every attribute. Where neither option fits, users can enter a custom value.

Source left, target right — scannable diff. Every field independently assignable. Custom value option means users aren't forced to pick either.

> 07 · THE_HARDEST_PROBLEM
Nothing moves silently.

This was the most debated part of the entire project. When two suppliers merge, what happens to their subsidiaries, affiliates, parent companies, and joint ventures? Auto-assigning relationships felt too risky — one silent move could corrupt an entire supplier hierarchy.

BEFORE — Early design
  • Bulk assignment — select all and move
  • One misclick moves everything silently
  • No visibility into what moved where
AFTER — Final design
  • Every relationship listed individually
  • One explicit decision per record
  • No defaults, no assumptions

A mandatory confirmation checkbox at the bottom requires users to acknowledge they've reviewed every assignment before the flow can proceed. This isn't friction for friction's sake — it's a deliberate signal that what follows is irreversible without an audit trail.

One misclick here corrupts an entire hierarchy. That's why nothing is assumed.

Step 4 — Review and Merge

A complete summary of the merged supplier — all attributes, contacts, locations, and a relationship assignment table showing exactly where every record ended up. Users can navigate back and correct anything before executing.

Before you execute, you see everything. That's not a courtesy — it's the design.

> 08 · SPLIT_FLOW

The inverse problem.
Same rigour.

The split flow solves the inverse problem — a supplier that was incorrectly merged needs to be separated. The structure mirrors the merge flow intentionally, so users who've done a merge already have a mental model they can apply.

Same entry point. Same mental model. No relearning.

Chunked by type — Contacts, then Locations, then Documents. One assign action per item. Nothing assumed.

Each new supplier is reviewable in its own tab before the split executes. Same mandatory confirmation checkbox as merge — consistent, deliberate, logged.

Same rigour as merge. Because the stakes are identical.

> 10 · LIMITATIONS

What we didn't
get to solve — yet.

No usability testing with real users.

Designs were based on stakeholder inputs and competitive analysis — not yet validated with actual data stewards.

Bulk operations not designed.

Power users managing hundreds of records may need faster ways to assign relationships in bulk. Phase 2 problem.

Mobile experience unconsidered.

The platform is desktop-only for now. Mobile use cases for data stewards remain unexplored.

Phase 1 ships. Phase 2 learns. That's how good products are built.

> 11 · REFLECTION

Designing for trust is different from designing for usability.

Usability asks: can the user do this?
Trust asks: does the user feel safe doing this?

The relationship assignment screen isn't the most beautiful thing I've designed. It might be the most important.

Not because it looks good. Because it makes the whole system trustworthy. That's the kind of designer I'm trying to be.

Two formats, one story. Recruiter chooses their depth.
